<p id="ir1">As it approaches the eighth anniversary of its release, the Garmin inReach Mini remains the gold standard for many hikers. Over the years, I have bought countless pieces of gear and equipment. Unlike many modern hikers who carry sophisticated electronics such as drones or high-end cameras, I have historically prioritized simplicity over technological complexity.   But this little orange and black satellite communicator is a no-brainer and by far the best money I have spent on hiking gear.</p>

![](/images/blog/in6.jpeg)

<p id=ir2">To be clear, this isn’t a traditional review—there are already hundreds of those online for anyone looking for a spec sheet. Instead, this is an appreciation for a unit and a brand that simply got it right. **Of course, it will inevitably sound like a review anyway; it’s hard to talk about gear this reliable without sounding like a fan**. So, let's go. 

- 2.04” x 3.90” x 1.03
- 3.5 oz
- IPX7
- Rechargeable internal lithium-ion

Up to 90 hours at 10-minute tracking (default)
Up to 35 hours at 10-minute tracking with 1-second logging
Up to 24 days at 30-minute tracking power save mode
Up to 1 year when powered off

Satellite coverage is generally reliable when you have a clear blue sky and when you aren't obscured by thick,  tall vegetations. I have used them in Ecuador, Bolivia and the Philippines without a single issue. Garmin's subscription vary and subject to change depending on the company policy, but the peace of mind remains constant.</p>

![](/images/blog/in7.jpeg)

<p id="ir3>Since the initial release of the original, two subsequent models have already available. An upgrade that enticing to others such as longer battery life, better mobile application, better satellite acquisition and now with larger touchscreen. But the core use is still the same. I believe that every future model will be continue to be built on that same foundation.</p>

![](/images/blog/in8.jpeg)

<p id="ir4">Should I replace my old unit for the new model? Maybe. But at the moment, my inReach Mini still performs the tasks that truly matter: two-way messaging via satellite and an SOS button for when the situation arises. Make no mistake, a rescue operation is not like it is in the movies. It takes time, planning, and immense effort; it is never instant. Still, knowing that someone is out there looking for you—equipped with your location and the ability to message you back—provides a tremendous amount of hope and peace of mind. Five years later, my  $299 investment, plus the cost of the subscription, remains completely justifiable.
