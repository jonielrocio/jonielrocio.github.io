By: Joniel Rocio

In an era where everything is readily available, so much of what we own has become disposable. There is no doubt that our environmental impact is massive—especially on our soil when these items are not properly recycled. One fast-rising problem is electronic waste, or e-waste. According to the Global E-Waste Monitor 2024, e-waste is growing five times faster than documented recycling efforts. This is largely driven by shorter product life cycles and a lack of repair options. As a low-impact hiker, I take pride in applying trail ethics to my tech. With that joy, I present to you my sixteen-year-old 'Tank': a 2010 Apple MacBook Pro.

![](/images/blog/mbp1.jpg)

As always, we go back to the numbers. My MacBook Pro (13-inch, Mid 2010) originally featured a 2.4GHz Intel Core 2 Duo processor, 4GB of 1067 MHz DDR3 memory, and NVIDIA GeForce 320M graphics. It started with a 256GB SATA hard drive, but like any electronic device that has seen better days, I eventually upgraded it to a 1TB SSD. Back then, I paid north of $1,700 with Microsoft Office installed. The 'Unibody' aluminum design, the backlit keyboard, and that iconic illuminating Apple logo were the height of laptop luxury. Sixteen years is a long time to remember, but I still recall how fast and responsive it felt. At the time, the ultimate selling point was that macOS rarely caught viruses compared to the competition.

![](/images/blog/mbp3.jpg)

Back then, its usage was primarily for web surfing, Skype, and watching Netflix—long before the era of Smart TVs. I even used it for Facebook (YES, back when phone apps were not yet the norm), along with emails and various games I can no longer remember. In those early years, it was a typical laptop computer, just like any other one out there. You might ask: What is the significance of this machine’s history if it started out just like the others? Well, as I mentioned—and I hope it is clear—mine is still functioning. However, that longevity has come with an inevitable change in performance that has required a different approach to how I use it today.

![](/images/blog/mbp5.jpg)

The sixteen-year journey of this machine has been a long one. As information technology and cyberspace have continued their relentless march forward, my MacBook has become a 'granny' in these modern times. Lag is now a common companion, the battery life is objectively horrible, and the weight certainly doesn’t meet today’s standards for portability. The performance is simply not what it once was; it is the inevitable reality of the 'Law of Obsolescence.' Yet, even with these limitations, there is a distinct dignity in its continued service.

![](/images/blog/mbp4.jpg)

As a community and as individuals, we can all do our part to help eliminate e-waste. The first step is to identify, understand, and accept that we have a problem; the data is readily available online for anyone to see. While recycling is a step in the right direction, it is only a partial solution. My own way of contributing is through due diligence: choosing the right product and maintaining it properly. This combination is the key to a sustainable environment. In closing, I will stand by my belief that what Apple achieved with this machine is absolutely awesome—both for the consumer and the environment alike. Thank you, Apple, for building something that actually lasts. 

“P.S. If anyone at Apple is reading this and wants to see if the 2026 models can actually outlast 'The Tank,' my inbox is open! Until then, I’ll keep hitting 'commit' on the best laptop I’ve ever owned.”
