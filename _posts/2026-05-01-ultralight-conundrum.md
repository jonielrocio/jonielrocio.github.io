<h id="con">Ultralight: The Conundrum</h>

```c
Long before backpacking became a recreational hobby, indigenous peoples
worldwide developed specialized packs and lightweight gear for long-distance
trade and seasonal migration. As the Industrial Revolution and urbanization 
transformed the world, they sparked a collective desire to return to nature. 
This gave birth to hiking and backpacking as recreation and sport.
```

```c
With this rising passion, the demand for equipment skyrocketed. In the late
1800s, Col. Henry Merriam designed a knapsack for the U.S. Army; though innovative,
it was notoriously uncomfortable. At the time, most civilian hikers still relied on 
heavy canvas bags or simple "bindles."
```

```c
As popularity grew, designers began creating new packs for mass production. In the
early 1920s, Lloyd F. Nelson invented the external frame pack, utilizing a wooden
frame and canvas. His design was a milestone, focused primarily on hiker comfort.
```

```c
In the post-WWII era, pack designs saw continuous upgrades. Materials such as
aircraft-grade aluminum for frames and nylon for bags were introduced, making
equipment significantly lighter and more durable. Then, in 1967, Greg Lowe created
the first internal frame backpack. This allowed the pack to contour to the body,
improving balance and movement on technical terrain.
```

```c
We have now entered a new millennium where materials are lighter, more durable,
and more accessible than ever. Welcome to the rising trend of “ultralight” backpacking,
where every ounce on your back matters. Of course, the lighter the load, the better—I won't
debate anyone on that. I once carried a 75-liter pack on an overnight trip; God only knows
how heavy it was, as weighing packs wasn't common back then. In those days, a heavy pack was
often equated to masculinity and ego.
```

```c
As we age, we become wiser. We develop the character and knowledge to cover long distances
while minimizing the physical impact on our bodies. Most of us eventually trim the weight
we carry—moving from light packing to ultralight, and now to the emerging "super-ultralight."
```

```c
But the question I want to ask is: When is ultralight actually ultralight? Is it defined by
a specific number? Is it the community? The brands? The hype? Or, fundamentally, should the
definition be relative to the individual?
```
